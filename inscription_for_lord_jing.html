This is a transcription of dirge from the Han dynasty(206BCE-220 CE), edited by Avery Clare.

Source Description

The transcription of this inscription is adapted from Hanwei liuchao beike jiaozhu漢魏六朝碑刻校注 compiled by Mao Yuanming毛遠明, and Hanbei quanji漢碑全集 compiled by Xu Yuli徐玉立. The inscription was established in the Ren County任城 in 143 AD. The main content of the stele inscription is written in clerical script隸書. The epigraph title is written in seal script篆書. Each column contains 33 characters.

Inscription for Han Former Governor of Yi Region and Counselor of Beihai Commandery Lord Jing

In the second year of Han'an era (143 C.E.)

autumn □□

Lord Jing, former governor of Rencheng County in Beihai passed away.

Alas, how lamentable!

05    The state □□ treasure

The outstanding scholars lost their peers.

All the constellations lost the essence

The junior scholars and pupils are late in time.

From where does the Heaven

10    Arrange the Fate and confer the term.

There is life and there is death

Heaven indeed controls it.

How could that benevolent sage

Be subdued and not being left.

15    Hence the former subordinates and students

discussed with each other, saying:

As for the great figures of ancient times

None of which failed to shed light □ infinity,

Spread fragrance and radiance to the records and pieces.

20    Even if their bodies died, their deeds remain clear

Even if their parts perished, their namees survive.

Some have their figures pictured on the graphs,

Some have their hymns stroked on the winds and strings.

The latecomers chant their blazing meritious achievements,

25    The documents on bamboos slips and silk recount their feats.

Thus we composed the dirge, which says:

Prostrating and thinking of the Enlightened Repositor,

Who received his nature from Heaven.

Faithful and deeply virtuous,

30    He led the rituals and followed benevolence.

He investigated the Way and examined the norms,

He held the pureness and kept the authentic nature.

Bright and white, clear and upright

He restrained his self and cultivated his morality.

35    Truly he was gentle and truly he was firm,

Thus he was versed in martial arts and versed in letters.

He received official nomination from the Receptionist,

And informally served the Chamberlain of National Treasury.

Shedding his virtue to Yuan County,

40    He promoted profits and benefitted the people.

The violent officials thus changed their behaviors,

The trivial and feeble ones were covered in benevolence.

The authority established, the beneficence scattered,

He cultivated conduct as if he was a deity.

45    The emperor admired his deeds,

Appointed him to official positions with the tally and decree.

To guard the Yizhou Commandery,

The road was far distant and he was attached to his parents.

He bent to the elders and modestly declined

50    Day and night serving the imperial court.

He offered loyal and upright advice in the official documents,

He discerned the ritual order for the spring ploughing.

The imperial signet superadded the official appointment,

He occupied the position of the Chancellor of Beihai

55    He governed nineteen cities,

The neighboring countries inclined toward (him).

Clearly telling the good from the wicked,

He prioritized respect and concession.

The demolished and the falsified ones changed their minds,

60    The frivolous and cunning ones escaped from the border.

The ominous owls ceased to cry,

The parted sons returned to serve the parents.

The multitudes of the widowers and the widows,

Gained peace because of the blessings.

65    He accumulated the Way and cultivated his virtue,

□ blessedness to achieve prosperity.

As for the myriad beautiful rituals,

The Enlightened Repositor carried them out.

As for Benevolence and righteousness, the Way and the tactics,

70    The Enlightened Repositor embraced them.

As for Huang Zhu and Father Shao,

The Enlightened Repositor joined to be the third of them.

The official position of the Three Dukes and the buttresses,

The Enlightened Repositor is suitable for them.

75    While ill he was summoned,

He entrusted the official post and resigned.

His people □ thought of him ardently,

Far and near, they scratched their heads.

The farmers let go of the plows,

80    The merchants left the markets empty,

They followed his carriage and swallowed their tears.

How come the imperial court,

Deprived me of my caring father?

Within ten days after he left the official post,

85    His disease became critical and difficult.

The substance of jade-tablet and jade-disc,

Could not be retrieved on the verge of death.

The voices of the woeful whining were nonstop,

He passed away suddenly with no delay.

90    The filial sons crashed and collided,

Toppled and broken as if he was torn apart.

No longer could he be awaken,

He has returned forever to the underworld.

The villagers of the households and people in the prefecture,

95    Ran to the funeral with tears dripping.

The former subordinates were bereft,

Sobbing and hovering, pacing up and down.

The high officials of hats and canopies within the four seas,

Were startled with grief and full of sorrow.

100   That which has the Great Fate fixed,

Truly it is only conferred by Heaven.

The enlightened ruler set up the position,

The Enlightened Repositor would not take it.

The vassals and sons desire to serve,

105   The Enlightened Repositor will not remain.

Alas, how lamentable!

The coda says:

Examining the accumulation and the dusky tomb,

To express the extreme □. Oh!

110   □□□□,

Soared the spirit of Court Gentleman for Consultation. Oh!

Once again appointed the tiger general,

To pacify the common people. Oh!

The norm-like stratagems and rule-like advice,

115   Prioritized serving with allegiance and trustworthiness. Oh!

Assisted the guardians and defended the shields,

Appeasing the myriad people. Oh!

□□□□,

His grace is more and more brimful. Oh!

120   He should be promoted to join the tripod vassals,

To fasten the trunk of the pine. Oh!

Not eternal is his lifespan,

He left his subordinates and sons behind. Oh!

The benevolence reaches Bohai and Mountain Tai,

125   People composed Gan Tang for him. Oh!

Engraved on the stone and carved the inscriptions,

□ would not perish. Oh!

Han Gu Yizhou Taishou Beihai Xiang Jing Jun Ming漢故益州太守北海相景君銘1

Yizhou taishou益州太守 (Governor of Yizhou Commandery): Yizhou commandery (Yizhou jun郡) was a subordinate administrative unit of the Yi Region (Yizhou益州) located in roughly the Sichuan Province today. The Han local government was carried out in a Region-Commandery-District (zhou州-jun郡-xian縣) system. The Han empire was divided into thirteen Regions (zhou州), under each Regions were Commanderies(jun) administrated by Governors (taishou太守; grand administrator), as the title and the text of this inscription indicates: 守郡益州.

See Hou Hanshu, zhi 23. 3512.

Beihai xiang北海相 (Chancellor of the Beihai): Beihai was a Princedom (wangguo王國) corresponding in size to commanderies in the Han time. The Han carried out a semifeudal character to government, equally divided the regional units administrated by the central government as Commanderies (jun郡) and the areas granted to the princes and marquises with feudal titles as Princedoms (guo).

Xiang相 (Chancellor, Administrator, or Counselor-delegate) in the Han time, according to Hucker, was “the senior official in a Princedom, Marquisate, or other semi feudal domain.” This official position is equivalent with a Governor (taishou) of the Commandery (jun) in the Han time.

See Hou Hanshu, zhi 22. 3473. Charles O. Hucker, A Dictionary of Official Titles in Imperial China. (Stanford, Calif: Stanford University Press, 1985), 230; Hans Bielenstein, “The Institutions of Later Han,” in The Cambridge History of China, ed. Denis Twitchett and Michael Loewe (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1986), 506–508.

假階司農4

Sinong司農 (Chamberlain for the National Treasury), also known as Da sinong大司農, was one of the Nine Chamberlains (jiuqing九卿) in the central government, administering the financial issues including the granary system and salt and iron in the Han dynasty. Lord Jing probably informally served in the central government first and then went to serve the local governments, as indicated in the text.

See Hucker, A Dictionary of Official Titles in Imperial China, 13, 453.

辨秩東衍6

This is a variant of the quote from the Book of Documents, 平秩東作. According to the commentary, the year starts from Spring and the ploughing in the farmland begins. There were also ritual sacrifices associated with the spring ploughing, thus Lord Jing was serving the role to ‘discern order’ (bianzhi辨秩) for this rite. In ancient Chinese culture, dong東(east) is also associated with Spring as East is the direction where Spring begins.

See Hou Hanshu, 40.1368.

部城十九7

The number nineteen (shijiu十九) of the subordinate units County (cheng城) is different from the treatises in Hou Hanshu (eighteen). A tentative explanation is that the Western Jin (265-316) historian Sima Biao司馬彪 (d.306) as the compiler of the treatise probably did not take Beihai county itself into account.

See Hou Hanshu, zhi 22. 3473.
